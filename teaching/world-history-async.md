# Overview

This course explores the processes that shaped global history from 1500 to the turn of the twenty-first century. We’ll work together to interpret primary and secondary sources and develop evidence-based arguments about the past and our ever-changing world.

---

# Themes

Everything is shaped by what came before. Thinking historically means attending to how and why things change over time, and in this course, we’ll take that approach as we seek to understand the historical processes that shaped our contemporary world.

In particular, we’ll explore the processes by which once-distant regions have become entangled by empire, migration, commerce, and the circulation of ideas. To do so, we’ll examine primary sources, including first-hand recollections, institutional pronouncements, films, and maps.

The goals of this course are ambitious; no single framework can adequately account for such a broad sweep of human history. I welcome your constructive feedback.

# Goals

This class is part of the revised UALR Core curriculum, which includes several Core Competencies/Learning Outcomes:

1. **Written Communication:** Write clearly and effectively using language appropriate to the discipline of history.  
2. **Oral Communication:** Present ideas and arguments clearly and effectively using language appropriate to the discipline of history.  
3. **Knowledge/Critical Thinking:** Analyze connections between historical events and interactions among different groups and societies.  
4. **Technology:** Use technology to locate and evaluate relevant primary & secondary historical sources.  
5. **Ethical Reasoning:** Recognize diverse perspectives when analyzing historical events and contexts.

# Discussion (Perusall)

In this course, we will use the online tool “Perusall” to read and annotate primary sources as a class.

Perusall serves as a venue to ask for clarification about a term or passage and share your thoughts with your peers.

Your grade in this category will depend on:
- frequency of participation
- engagement with peers
- quality of comments and questions

Perusall does not yet have a functioning mobile app, so you must use a desktop computer, tablet, or laptop.

---

# Exhibit Assignments

We will create two interactive digital exhibits:

1. A pictorial-map exhibit using the David Rumsey Map Collection  
2. A photo exhibit using the Allison WWII Photographs Collection

Each exhibit must include:
- at least 250 words of written content
- historical contextualization
- primary-source analysis
- a 1.5–3 minute audio or video walkthrough

Students will peer review one another’s exhibits and revise accordingly.

# Academic Integrity

## Plagiarism

All sources, including material gathered online, must be properly cited.

Students involved in plagiarism or cheating may receive the lowest possible grade for the assignment and may be referred for disciplinary action.

## Generative AI

Submitting work generated fully or substantially by AI tools (including ChatGPT) constitutes academic dishonesty.

AI may appear useful, but it is not a compelling author. Trust your own interpretive voice.
